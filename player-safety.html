Player Safety

Our goal is to tell exciting, challenging stories together while making sure that fictional challenges do not cause real-life discomfort or distress. Think of Lines and Veils as the blueprint for our shared comfort zone.

Back to Main Page

Why This Matters

Good boundaries make better games.

Table safety is not about removing tension, horror, or drama from a game. It is about making sure everyone can enjoy those things without being blindsided by content that crosses a personal boundary. Setting expectations up front makes it easier for the group to relax, take risks in character, and trust one another in play.

The goal: create a shared understanding of what kinds of content are welcome, what should stay off-screen, and what should stay out of the game entirely.

What Are Lines and Veils?

Clear boundaries help everyone stay in the game.

Lines and Veils are a simple, effective tool for setting content boundaries before play begins. They give everyone a way to say what should be excluded completely and what can exist in the story only off-screen.

Lines (The Hard “No”)

A Line is a topic, image, or theme that will not appear in the game at all. If something is a Line, we do not include it directly, imply it, or use it as background flavor.

Example: “Violence against children is a Line for me. I do not want it mentioned or implied.”

Veils (Fade to Black)

A Veil is a topic that can exist in the story, but the moment it would come on-screen, we fade to black. We acknowledge that it happened without roleplaying through it or describing it in detail.

Example: “Sexual violence is a Veil. It can exist as part of the world or backstory, but the scene cuts away immediately and is never described.”

Important: I will use an anonymous process, such as a private message or simple form, to collect everyone’s Lines and Veils before we begin character creation. I will then share a group summary focused on the topics, not on who requested them.

Examples of Topics Players May Want to Flag

Prompts to help people think through boundaries.

Not every player will react the same way to the same material. These examples are here to help people identify possible concerns, not to assume that every topic is automatically off-limits.

Common Content & Situational Triggers
Physical Violence
  • Torture, especially detailed descriptions
  • Graphic or excessive gore and body horror
  • Dismemberment or amputation
Interpersonal Conflict
  • Non-consensual sexual activity or references
  • Physical, emotional, or verbal abuse
  • Self-harm or suicide, including references to it
Illness & Injury
  • Sickness or disease, especially real-world epidemic themes
  • Detailed descriptions of major or permanent injury
Mental & Emotional States
  • Detailed descriptions of intense fear or phobias
  • Mental illness or breakdown, especially when handled insensitively
Death & Loss
  • Death of children or animals
  • Specific methods of death, such as drowning, burning, or starvation
Less Common & More Specific Narrative Triggers
Substance Use
  • Drug or alcohol addiction and abuse
Socio-Political Themes
  • Racism, bigotry, or xenophobia as a plot element
  • Slavery or human trafficking
  • Political or religious persecution, including witch-hunt themes
Environmental Triggers
  • Claustrophobia or tight spaces
  • Extreme heights or vertigo
Body & Autonomy
  • Body modification or mutation, especially non-magical versions
  • Mind control or loss of autonomy
Specific Scenario Concerns
  • Betrayal by trusted NPCs or allies
  • Unwinnable scenarios or total party kill (TPK)
Specific Horror & Dark Fantasy Themes

My games often include elements of cosmic horror, the macabre, and dark fantasy. What feels fun and spooky to one person can feel overwhelming or genuinely distressing to another, so these themes deserve special attention.

Cosmic Horror
  • Existential hopelessness or nihilism
  • Detailed depictions of madness or insanity
  • Loss of memory or identity
  • Unnatural or non-Euclidean geometry/architecture
Undead & Gore
  • Detailed descriptions of rotting or decaying flesh
  • Cannibalism
  • Zombies, ghouls, or skeletons
Demonic & Infernal Themes
  • Possession or exorcism
  • Pacts or deals with evil entities
  • Sacrifice, especially detailed ritual sacrifice
Body Horror
  • Parasites or infestation
  • Rapid, uncontrollable mutation

Safety Tools Beyond Lines and Veils

Boundaries are set up front, but safety continues during play.

Even with clear Lines and Veils, unexpected moments can come up in play. These tools give everyone a simple way to slow down, redirect a scene, or check in without needing to justify themselves in the moment.

Pause or Check-In Language

Any player can say something simple like “Pause”, “Can we check in?”, or “Can we pull back a bit?” if a scene is getting too intense. We stop, adjust, and continue without anyone needing to explain more than they want to.

The X-Card

The X-Card is a simple signal that says, “This content needs to stop or change.” It can be a literal card on the table, a typed “X” in chat, or any other agreed signal. When it is used, we move on immediately with no debate.

Open Door Policy

Anyone can step away from the table for any reason at any time. No one is required to stay in a scene that is upsetting, overwhelming, or simply too much that day.

Aftercare and Debrief

After intense sessions, we can take a few minutes to decompress, talk about what worked, and note anything that should be avoided or handled differently in the future.

Bottom line: safety tools are not interruptions to the game. They are part of how we make the game work for everyone.

How This Works at the Table

Safety is ongoing, not one-and-done.

  • We set Lines and Veils before the campaign begins.
  • Anyone can add a new Line or Veil later if something unexpected comes up.
  • If a boundary is crossed, we stop, adjust, and move on without argument or pressure.
  • No one has to explain or defend why a topic is uncomfortable.
  • Respecting boundaries is part of being a good player and a good GM.

The goal is not to remove horror, drama, danger, or difficult choices from play. The goal is to make sure those elements stay fictional and fun, rather than becoming real-life distress.
